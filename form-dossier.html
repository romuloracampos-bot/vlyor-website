Product Overview

FormEssence O Series is a range of fatty alcohol ethoxylates (FAE) produced by ethoxylation of C12–C18 linear fatty alcohols with 3 to 50 ethylene oxide (EO) units. The degree of ethoxylation determines the HLB of the resulting molecule — each additional EO unit adds approximately 0.5 HLB units — enabling precise selection of emulsifier character for any formulation requirement.

Low-EO grades (O-3, O-5, HLB 6–8) function as W/O co-emulsifiers and wetting agents. Mid-EO grades (O-8 to O-10, HLB 12–14) are primary O/W emulsifiers for skin care creams and lotions, optimal for emulsifying mineral oils, fatty esters and silicone fluids. High-EO grades (O-20 to O-50, HLB 16–19) transition to solubiliser function, incorporating fragrance, essential oils and fat-soluble vitamins into clear aqueous systems without turbidity.

Mechanism — Ethoxylate Emulsification and HLB Theory

The emulsification mechanism of fatty alcohol ethoxylates is governed by the hydrophilic-lipophilic balance (HLB) of the molecule. The lipophilic C12–C18 fatty alcohol chain anchors into the oil phase droplet; the polyethylene glycol (PEG) chain extends into the aqueous continuous phase. The relative chain lengths determine whether the molecule more effectively stabilises oil-in-water (high-EO, hydrophilic PEG dominates) or water-in-oil (low-EO, lipophilic chain dominates) emulsions. At the optimal concentration (typically 2–8%), the emulsifier molecules form a tightly packed monolayer at the oil-water interface, reducing interfacial tension from ~35 mN/m to <1 mN/m and providing steric and electrostatic repulsion between droplets that prevents coalescence. The non-ionic character (no charge) provides compatibility across the full cosmetic pH range (3–10) and insensitivity to electrolytes — an advantage over soap-based emulsifiers whose emulsification degrades at low pH.

Product Overview

FormEssence EL Series comprises ethoxylated castor oil (EL grades) and ethoxylated hydrogenated castor oil (HEL grades). The triricinolein backbone of castor oil provides the lipophilic anchor; the polyethylene glycol chain length (10–90 EO) determines HLB and solubilisation capacity. The critical distinction between EL and HEL grades is oxidation stability: EL grades use unsaturated castor oil fatty acids (ricinoleic acid, C18:1-OH) while HEL grades use the fully hydrogenated, saturated equivalent.

HEL grades are preferred for sensitive formulations — the saturated hydroxystearic acid chains are significantly more resistant to oxidative degradation than the unsaturated ricinoleic chains of EL grades. HEL-40 (PEG-40 Hydrogenated Castor Oil, CAS 61788-85-0) is the pharmaceutical and cosmetic standard for solubilising fat-soluble vitamins (Vitamin E, A, D3) and essential oils in clear aqueous systems, meeting Ph.Eur. “Macrogolglycerol hydroxystearate” and USP/NF “Polyoxyl 40 Hydrogenated Castor Oil” monographs. Critical micelle concentration 0.03% w/w at 37°C; micelles 10–15 nm diameter in aqueous systems.

Mechanism — Triricinoleate Backbone and Solubilisation

The solubilisation power of castor oil ethoxylates derives from the triricinolein backbone structure: three esterified fatty acid chains (versus one in sorbitan monoesters) create a more voluminous lipophilic core within the surfactant micelle. This larger micelle interior solubilises a greater mass of lipophilic material — fragrance compounds, essential oils, vitamins — at lower surfactant loading than equivalent single-chain ethoxylates (e.g., fatty alcohol ethoxylates or polysorbates). At the HLB values of HEL-40 (HLB ~14), the surfactant forms relatively large micelles in water (10–15 nm diameter) with a substantial lipophilic core. Lipophilic solubilisates (Vitamin E, fragrance) partition into this core with high affinity, producing clear aqueous solutions at vitamin loadings of 3–10% — concentrations that would produce turbid or phase-separated systems with lower-capacity solubilisers.

Product Overview

SorbEssence S-20 is Sorbitan Monolaurate — the C12 (lauric acid) ester of sorbitan. Sorbitan (1,4-anhydrosorbitol) is a cyclic dehydration product of sorbitol, providing the polyol backbone for esterification with fatty acids. With HLB 8.6, it is the most hydrophilic member of the sorbitan ester family, positioned at the boundary between wetting agent function (HLB 7–9) and O/W co-emulsifier function (HLB 8–12).

When combined with Polysorbate 20 (HLB 16.7), SorbEssence S-20 enables stable O/W emulsions via the sorbitan ester/polysorbate HLB blending approach. The matched C12 laurate chain in both emulsifiers — S-20 in the oil phase and polysorbate at the interface — creates the most synergistic mixed interfacial film of any laurate emulsifier pair. As a standalone in lipophilic formulations, it reduces interfacial tension, improves pigment and powder wetting and acts as a spreading agent.

Mechanism — Sorbitan Ester Emulsification

Sorbitan esters are produced by the internal dehydration of sorbitol under high temperature (>200°C) to form sorbitan, followed by esterification with fatty acids. The resulting molecule has a polar sorbitan ring head group (four —OH groups partially esterified) and one to three lipophilic fatty acid chains. The HLB of 8.6 for the C12 monolaurate reflects the balance between the polar sorbitan head (hydrophilic) and the single C12 chain (lipophilic). At the oil-water interface in an emulsion, the fatty acid chain anchors into the oil phase while the sorbitan head group orients toward the aqueous phase, forming a packed interfacial film. The short C12 laurate chain makes this the most polar sorbitan monolaurate — maximum interfacial packing efficiency with polysorbate counterparts for O/W co-emulsification.

Product Overview

SorbEssence S-40 is Sorbitan Monopalmitate — the C16 (palmitic acid) ester of sorbitan. The C16 saturated palmitate chain produces a waxy solid at room temperature (melting point approximately 45–50°C), providing both emulsification at the oil-water interface and structural / consistency contribution to wax-based formulations. At HLB 6.7, it is firmly positioned in the W/O emulsifier range.

In W/O emulsions, S-40 occupies an intermediate position between the more hydrophilic S-20 (HLB 8.6) and the more lipophilic S-60 (HLB 4.7), providing formulating flexibility for oil phases with intermediate required HLB. Its waxy solid state at room temperature also provides structural reinforcement at the interfacial film — the physical rigidity of the crystalline C16 chain contributes to emulsion stability by increasing the mechanical resistance of the interfacial layer to deformation and coalescence under shear and temperature stress.

Mechanism — Crystalline Interfacial Film Reinforcement

The C16 palmitate chain of sorbitan monopalmitate has a melting point of approximately 63°C in the pure fatty acid form. In sorbitan ester form, the melting point is reduced to ~45–50°C by the bulky sorbitan head group disrupting crystalline packing. At temperatures below this melting point, some proportion of the interfacial sorbitan ester molecules exist in a semi-crystalline state at the emulsion droplet surface, creating a rigid, two-dimensional crystalline network that mechanically resists droplet coalescence. This Pickering-like interfacial film reinforcement is the primary mechanism by which waxy solid sorbitan esters (S-40, S-60) produce more stable W/O emulsions than fluid sorbitan esters (S-20, S-80) at equivalent HLB values — the physical rigidity of the solid emulsifier adds mechanical stability absent in liquid emulsifier systems.

Product Overview

SorbEssence S-60 is Sorbitan Monostearate — the C18 (stearic acid) ester of sorbitan. With HLB 4.7 and a fully saturated C18 chain, it is the most commercially used solid sorbitan ester in personal care, widely specified for emulsification of fatty alcohols, fatty esters, mineral oils and silicone oils. Its melting point of ~50–55°C makes it suitable for hot-process incorporation where it contributes structural consistency to the formulation.

The defining commercial advantage of S-60 is its performance paired with Polysorbate 60 (HLB 14.9): the matched C18 stearate chain in both emulsifiers — S-60 in the W/O position and polysorbate at the interface — produces stable O/W emulsions with particularly pleasant conditioning aesthetics. The physically matched chain lengths create the most efficient packing of mixed interfacial film, reducing interfacial tension to the lowest level achievable with sorbitan ester systems. The resulting emulsion texture is characteristically rich, smooth and conditioning — the standard for premium conditioning cream formulations.

Mechanism — Matched Chain-Length Interfacial Film and Conditioning Aesthetics

The mechanism by which the C18 stearate Span/Polysorbate pair produces superior aesthetics versus other chain lengths relates to the molecular geometry of interfacial film packing. When both the sorbitan ester (W/O position) and the polysorbate (O/W position) carry the same fatty acid chain length, the two molecules can interdigitate their hydrocarbon chains at the interface in a crystallographically favourable arrangement — analogous to a zipper closing. This molecular complementarity reduces the area per molecule at the interface, increases the viscoelastic modulus of the interfacial film, and reduces coalescence kinetics. The practical result is smaller, more uniform droplet size distribution, which the skin translates as a richer, creamier, more conditioning sensory experience. The C18 saturated stearate chain provides additional benefits: no oxidative instability risk, no contribution to off-odour development, and a distinct waxy melting behaviour at ~50–55°C that contributes to emulsion stability through gel network formation at storage temperatures.

Product Overview

SorbEssence S-80 is Sorbitan Monooleate — the C18:1 (oleic acid, unsaturated) ester of sorbitan. Three properties account for its global market dominance: HLB 4.3 sits in the centre of the W/O emulsifier range required by most mineral, silicone and vegetable oil phases (required HLB typically 3–5); the unsaturated C18:1 oleate chain prevents crystallisation at ambient temperature, maintaining liquid state without heating requirements; and the oleic acid chain provides outstanding compatibility with unsaturated lipid components — vegetable oils, oleyl alcohol, liquid fatty acids — that are incompatible with the saturated C16/C18 chains of S-40 and S-60.

The liquid state is a critical processing advantage: S-80 can be pumped, metered and incorporated into batch processes at room temperature without heated dosing lines, reducing energy consumption and the risk of volatile component loss during hot-process manufacturing. Combined with Polysorbate 80 (HLB 15.0), S-80 creates the most versatile single sorbitan ester/polysorbate pair in the cosmetic industry — by adjusting the S-80:Polysorbate 80 ratio, any blended HLB from 4.3 to 15.0 can be achieved, covering the complete range from W/O to O/W emulsification with a single pair of emulsifiers.

Mechanism — Oleate Chain, Liquid State and Oil Phase Compatibility

The unsaturated C18:1 oleate chain of S-80 has a melting point of ~4°C (versus ~63°C for stearic C18:0), maintaining liquid state throughout normal handling and manufacturing temperatures. At the oil-water interface, the cis double bond at the 9–10 carbon position creates a kinked chain geometry that prevents close packing of adjacent emulsifier molecules — producing a less ordered, more fluid interfacial film than the crystalline interfacial films of saturated-chain S-40 and S-60. This fluid interface produces emulsions with lower viscosity and a lighter, more freely flowing character versus the solid-sorbitan ester emulsions. For vegetable oil and oleyl alcohol emulsification, the unsaturated oleate chain of S-80 provides favourable chain-chain interactions with the predominantly unsaturated fatty acid chains of the dispersed oil phase — reducing interfacial tension more effectively than saturated chain emulsifiers for these unsaturated oil systems.

Product Overview

SorbEssence S-85 is Sorbitan Trioleate — three oleic acid chains (C18:1) esterified to the sorbitan backbone. The tri-esterification of sorbitan with three oleic acid chains produces a highly lipophilic molecule with HLB 1.8 — at the extreme lipophilic end of the emulsifier spectrum. All three hydroxyl positions normally available for esterification on the sorbitan ring are occupied, leaving only one remaining —OH group (the ring oxygen) as the sole polar moiety. This minimal hydrophilicity positions S-85 firmly as a W/O emulsifier for highly non-polar continuous phases.

Mechanism — Tri-Esterification and Extreme Lipophilicity

The tri-esterification of sorbitan maximises the number of lipophilic fatty acid chains per molecule while minimising the number of free polar groups. A sorbitan monoester has three free —OH groups and one esterified fatty acid; a triester has only one free —OH group and three fatty acid chains. This fundamental structural change dramatically shifts the HLB from 8.6 (S-20) through 4.3 (S-80) to 1.8 (S-85) as esterification degree increases — more fatty acids reduce HLB. The three C18:1 oleate chains produce a voluminous lipophilic shell around the polar sorbitan core, making the molecule highly compatible with non-polar oils and solvents but essentially incompatible with water in the absence of co-emulsifiers or high-HLB surfactants. In aerosol W/O emulsions, the extreme lipophilicity of S-85 provides effective emulsification of the water droplet phase within non-polar hydrocarbon propellant continuous phases — a formulation environment where even HLB 4.3 emulsifiers (S-80) may be insufficiently lipophilic.

Mechanism — In-Situ Soap Emulsification

Mechanism — Rigid Rod Network Formation and Shear-Thinning

The exceptional rheological properties of Xanthan Gum arise from the molecular architecture of the polysaccharide chain. Product Overview

EsterSilk IPP is Isopropyl Palmitate (IPP) — the isopropyl ester of palmitic acid (C16). Structurally identical to IPM in its isopropyl ester head group, it differs in the fatty acid chain length: C16 palmitate versus C14 myristate. This two-carbon difference produces a marginally higher molecular weight and slightly higher viscosity (~8 mPa·s vs. ~5 mPa·s for IPM) — the C16 chain creates a slightly more substantive emollient character that remains firmly within the dry-touch emollient category.

IPP is most commonly used as a direct formulation alternative to IPM where a slightly richer emollient character is preferred without moving to heavier oils (natural oils, MCT) or silicone fluids. The choice between IPM and IPP frequently reflects specific skin feel briefs: IPM for maximum dryness and lightest texture; IPP for slightly more cushioned emolliency with better wash-off profile. In hair care, IPP provides improved cuticle coating substantivity versus IPM while maintaining the light, non-greasy character of the isopropyl ester class. Both remain far lighter and drier than equivalent loading of any natural oil or MCT.

Mechanism — Chain Length Effect on Emollient Character

The relationship between fatty acid chain length and emollient skin feel in isopropyl esters follows predictable physical-chemical principles. Longer chain fatty esters have higher molecular weights, lower vapour pressures, higher boiling points and slightly higher viscosities — all contributing to a more substantive, slower-evaporating emollient character. The C14 chain of IPM produces a very fast-spreading, rapidly-absorbed emollient with minimal skin persistence. The C16 chain of IPP produces a slightly slower spread rate and marginally greater skin persistence — the emollient is detectable on skin for slightly longer after application. In formulation terms, this means IPP provides a more cushioned initial skin feel (slightly more resistance during spreading) and a small but detectable improvement in skin softness after application versus IPM. However, both esters complete their absorption into the stratum corneum within minutes, leaving no visible or tactile residue on the skin surface. The C16 palmitate chain also has slightly lower comedogenicity potential than the C14 myristate chain, making IPP the preferred grade in acne-prone or sensitive skin formulations where comedogenicity is a clinical concern.

Product Overview

TricaPure is Caprylic/Capric Triglyceride (MCT) — a triglyceride ester of glycerol with C8 (caprylic) and C10 (capric) medium-chain saturated fatty acids, produced by fractionation of coconut or palm kernel oil followed by esterification with glycerol. Unlike long-chain natural oils (olive oil, argan oil, rosehip oil) which consist primarily of C18 fatty acids with varying degrees of unsaturation (susceptible to oxidative rancidity), MCT consists entirely of fully saturated C8/C10 fatty acids. This complete saturation produces a unique combination of properties that explains the exceptional commercial importance of MCT in the cosmetic industry.

The three commercially defining properties of TricaPure are: Non-greasy, light skin feel comparable to synthetic esters despite being a natural triglyceride — the short C8/C10 chains spread rapidly and absorb quickly into skin without the heavy residue of long-chain natural oils; Exceptional oxidation stability with no unsaturated bonds to oxidise and no rancidity risk, requiring no antioxidant addition and enabling extended product shelf-life without stability concerns; and Outstanding active carrier function — MCT dissolves oil-soluble vitamins (Vitamin E, D3, A), UV filters, lipophilic plant extracts and fragrance at high concentrations, making it the preferred carrier oil for active delivery in natural-positioned formulations where synthetic solvents are excluded from the ingredient brief.

Mechanism — Medium-Chain Triglyceride Properties and Active Carrier Function

The exceptional properties of MCT relative to long-chain natural oils arise from the physical chemistry of the C8/C10 fatty acid chains. Compared to the C18 chains of long-chain oils: C8/C10 chains are shorter (lower molecular weight) and fully saturated; shorter chains produce lower viscosity and faster spreading kinetics on skin; saturated chains eliminate the oxidative instability from double bonds; and the medium chain length produces optimal partitioning between the oil phase and the stratum corneum lipid matrix — neither too short (which would produce excessive volatility) nor too long (which would produce poor skin penetration). The active carrier function derives from the high lipophilicity of the MCT continuous phase: fat-soluble vitamins, UV filters and other lipophilic actives partition preferentially into the MCT oil phase from an aqueous emulsion, producing an effective reservoir of dissolved active in the oil droplet. When the emulsion is applied to skin, the oil droplet spreads and the dissolved active partitions into the stratum corneum lipid matrix in contact with the MCT droplet — a passive delivery mechanism that delivers lipophilic actives effectively without requiring penetration enhancement. The zero-unsaturation character of MCT also protects oxidation-sensitive actives (retinol, tocopherol) from oxidative degradation during formulation and shelf-life, extending effective active concentration in the finished product.

Product Overview

SqualEssence is Squalane (fully hydrogenated squalene) derived from olive oil squalene. Squalene is a natural constituent of human sebum — the skin's own lipid secretion — representing approximately 13% of sebum lipid composition. In the body, squalene functions as a natural emollient and antioxidant precursor (squalene is the biosynthetic precursor to all sterols, including cholesterol). When extracted from olive oil (which contains 0.1–0.7% squalene) and hydrogenated to the fully saturated squalane form, the compound retains complete biological affinity for human skin while eliminating the six double bonds of squalene that cause oxidative instability.

The skin-identical structure of squalane is the basis of its commercially unmatched emollient properties: it integrates seamlessly into the stratum corneum lipid matrix, occupying the same lipid domains as endogenous sebum squalane. This biological compatibility produces a skin feel that consumers consistently describe as ‘skin-like’ or ‘my skin but better’ — the hallmark of a skin-identical ingredient. SqualEssence also provides exceptional active stabilisation: squalane scavenges free radicals through the remaining structural features of its isoprene backbone, protecting oxidation-sensitive actives (retinol, Vitamin C derivatives, polyunsaturated fatty acids) during both formulation processing and shelf-life. Non-comedogenicity (comedogenicity rating 0) makes it appropriate for all skin types including acne-prone and sensitive.

Mechanism — Sebum Integration and Free Radical Scavenging

The exceptional skin compatibility of squalane derives from its biosynthetic origins and structural identity with human sebum. Human skin naturally produces squalene as an intermediate in the cholesterol biosynthesis pathway; keratinocytes and sebaceous glands secrete squalene as a component of sebum, where it serves as a natural emollient, antioxidant and potential UV filter. The squalane produced from olive oil is structurally identical to the squalane component of sebum — a fully saturated, branched isoprenoid hydrocarbon (six isoprene units, 30 carbons, highly branched structure). This structural identity allows topically applied squalane to integrate directly into the intercellular lipid matrix of the stratum corneum, where it occupies the same lipid domains as endogenous sebum squalane. Unlike synthetic esters or silicones which remain at the skin surface or penetrate only partially into the lipid matrix, squalane becomes part of the skin barrier structure itself — explaining the unique moisturisation and barrier reinforcement properties that no synthetic emollient replicates. The free radical scavenging activity of squalane — despite being fully saturated — arises from the quaternary carbon centres of the isoprenoid branching points, which can donate hydrogen atoms to free radical species, quenching oxidative chain reactions. This antioxidant activity protects both the skin from environmental oxidative stress and formulation actives (retinol, tocopherol) from oxidative degradation during shelf-life.
