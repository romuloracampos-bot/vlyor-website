Product Overview

AquaForm PEG Series is Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) — a family of linear polyether polymers produced by base-catalysed polymerisation of ethylene oxide with the molecular formula (C⊂2;H⊂4;O)⊂n;·H⊂2;O. The degree of polymerisation (n) determines molecular weight, physical form and consequent function. Liquid grades (PEG 200–600, MW 190–630 g/mol) are clear, colourless, slightly viscous liquids completely miscible with water and most polar organic solvents. Semi-solid grades (PEG 1000–1500) are pastes at ambient temperature. Solid grades (PEG 4000–8000, MP 55–65°C) are white waxy flakes or powders.

Liquid PEG grades (200–600) function primarily as humectants and solvents: their multiple ether oxygen atoms and terminal hydroxyl groups attract and bind water molecules through hydrogen bonding, drawing moisture from the environment into the formulation. PEG 400 is the most commercially used grade — its balanced molecular weight provides effective humectancy, excellent active solubilisation, low viscosity for processing, and outstanding compatibility with virtually every cosmetic ingredient class. Solid PEG grades (4000–8000) function as consistency factors, ointment bases and water-soluble wax alternatives — providing waxy structure and controlled-release moisturisation in anhydrous and low-water formulations.

Mechanism — Hydrogen Bond Water Binding and MW-Dependent Function

The humectant mechanism of PEG operates through hydrogen bonding between the polyether oxygen atoms along the PEG backbone and water molecules. Each ethylene oxide unit (—CH⊂2;—CH⊂2;—O—) contributes one ether oxygen capable of accepting a hydrogen bond from water — a PEG 400 molecule (approximately 9 EO units) can coordinate approximately 9 water molecules in its immediate hydration shell. The hygroscopicity of PEG decreases with increasing molecular weight: shorter chains are more hygroscopic (more EO units per unit mass relative to the less polar terminal groups); longer chains have proportionally fewer terminal hydroxyl groups and relatively more non-polar backbone character. This is why PEG 200 is more hygroscopic than PEG 8000. The physical form change (liquid to solid) with increasing MW is a consequence of increased van der Waals forces between longer polymer chains overcoming thermal energy at ambient temperature — the same crystalline packing that produces the waxy consistency of solid PEG grades.

Product Overview

HydraBase Glyc is Glycerin (Glycerol) — a trihydric alcohol (triol) with three hydroxyl groups per molecule and the molecular formula C⊂3;H⊂8;O⊂3;, CAS 56-81-5. Glycerin is the most commercially important humectant in personal care — the most versatile, the most effective and the most universally accepted. Its three —OH groups bind water molecules through hydrogen bonding with exceptional efficiency: at 50% relative humidity, glycerin absorbs approximately 25% of its own weight in water from the environment, the highest equilibrium hygroscopicity of any standard cosmetic humectant.

HydraBase Glyc is supplied to USP / BP / EP pharmacopoeial quality at minimum purity ≥99.5% from vegetable origin — primarily as a by-product of fatty acid / soap / biodiesel production from vegetable oils (palm, coconut, soy). This vegetable-derived origin enables natural-positioning and COSMOS-eligible formulations. The principal formulation management challenge is stickiness at high concentrations (>5–8% in leave-on products), managed through blending with PEG 400 or propylene glycol to reduce perceived tackiness without sacrificing water-binding capacity. In rinse-off products, 1–3% glycerin provides effective moisturisation without stickiness.

Mechanism — Trihydric Hydrogen Bonding and Water Binding

The superior hygroscopicity of glycerin relative to mono- and dihydric glycols derives directly from its three hydroxyl groups. Each —OH group is capable of donating and accepting hydrogen bonds: the hydrogen of —OH donates to water oxygen; the oxygen of —OH accepts hydrogen bonds from water hydrogens. In aqueous systems, a single glycerin molecule can coordinate up to 6 water molecules in its immediate hydrogen-bonding shell — significantly more than propylene glycol (2 hydroxyl groups) or PEG 400 (primarily ether oxygens). The hygroscopic equilibrium at 50% RH of 25% reflects this superior hydrogen-bonding capacity. In the stratum corneum, glycerin mimics the natural moisturising factor (NMF) — the skin's endogenous humectant system — contributing to barrier function and corneocyte hydration through the same hydrogen-bonding mechanism that operates in the bulk formulation.

Product Overview

HydraBase PG is Propylene Glycol (1,2-Propanediol) — the diol humectant and solvent with the molecular formula C⊂3;H⊂8;O⊂2;, CAS 57-55-6. It is the most widely used multifunctional cosmetic ingredient globally, present in the majority of commercial formulations as a humectant, active solubiliser, preservation system enhancer and penetration modifier simultaneously. Its low molecular weight (76 g/mol), very low viscosity (~56 mPa·s at 25°C) and outstanding compatibility across all cosmetic ingredient classes distinguish it from higher-MW glycols in terms of formulation versatility.

Four concurrent functions explain PG's commercial dominance: (1) Humectancy — water binding at ~20% w/w at 50% RH through its two hydroxyl groups, providing surface moisturisation without glycerin's stickiness; (2) Solubilisation — dissolving fragrance materials, lipophilic actives and preservatives that are insoluble in water or glycerin, improving their distribution throughout aqueous formulations; (3) Preservation enhancement — at concentrations above 5%, providing intrinsic antimicrobial activity that synergises with primary preservatives, enabling lower preservative loading; (4) Penetration enhancement — at 5–15%, transiently increasing stratum corneum permeability to improve delivery of co-formulated actives into the skin.

Mechanism — Diol Structure, Humectancy and Multifunctionality

The two hydroxyl groups of propylene glycol provide direct humectant activity through hydrogen bonding with water — each —OH accepts and donates hydrogen bonds to water molecules, binding water in the formulation and at the skin surface. Water binding at 50% RH (~20% w/w) is somewhat lower than glycerin (25%), reflecting the fewer hydroxyl groups per molecule (2 vs. 3). However, propylene glycol's lower molecular weight per —OH group (76 g/mol / 2 —OH = 38 g/mol per —OH) versus glycerin (92 / 3 = 31 g/mol) means the difference is modest, and PG provides better solvent properties and lower viscosity as compensation. The preservation enhancement mechanism operates through PG's ability to disrupt microbial cell membranes at concentrations above approximately 5% — acting as a co-preservative that reduces the minimum inhibitory concentration of primary preservatives by 20–40%, enabling more sustainable preservation formulation strategies.

Product Overview

HydraBase DPG is Dipropylene Glycol (DPG) — a mixture of three dipropylene glycol isomers produced as a by-product of propylene glycol manufacture, CAS 25265-71-8. Its molecular weight (134 g/mol) is approximately 1.75× that of propylene glycol (76 g/mol), producing a liquid with slightly higher viscosity (~110 mPa·s vs. ~56 mPa·s for PG) and a noticeably lighter, drier skin feel. This improved tactile profile makes DPG the preferred humectant for premium leave-on formulations where dry after-feel is a key sensory brief.

The primary commercial application of HydraBase DPG is as a fragrance carrier and diluent: its low odour, excellent solubilisation of both natural essential oils and synthetic aromatic molecules, and compatibility with all fragrance ingredient classes make it the industry standard for diluting concentrated fragrance materials before their addition to cosmetic formulations. As a fragrance carrier, DPG provides a stable, low-volatility matrix that releases fragrance gradually over time — improving longevity versus ethanol-based systems. In personal care products, DPG simultaneously provides humectancy and fragrance delivery function from a single ingredient addition.

Mechanism — MW Effect on Skin Feel and Fragrance Solvation

The lighter skin feel of DPG versus PG at equivalent concentration is a direct consequence of its higher molecular weight and consequently lower molar concentration per gram. At 5% by weight, DPG provides approximately 0.37 mmol/g of diol molecules versus PG's 0.66 mmol/g — fewer, larger molecules at equivalent mass loading. The fewer —OH groups per gram of DPG versus PG translate to reduced hydrogen-bond water binding (explaining the slightly lower humectancy) but also to reduced surface stickiness — the viscous, tacky feel of glycols is partially a consequence of their dense hydrogen-bonding network with the skin surface, which is less dense for DPG at equivalent mass loading. The fragrance solvation capacity of DPG arises from its combination of the hydroxyl groups (compatible with polar aromatic ring-containing molecules) and the ether oxygen (compatible with less polar aliphatic fragrance components) — providing a medium polarity solvent environment suitable for the diverse polarity range of fragrance raw materials.

Product Overview

HydraBase PDO is 1,3-Propanediol (Propanediol) — a diol isomeric to Propylene Glycol, CAS 504-63-2. The molecular formula is identical to PG (C⊂3;H⊂8;O⊂2;), but hydroxyl group positioning at carbons 1 and 3 rather than 1 and 2 produces different physical-chemical properties: lower viscosity, lighter skin feel, reduced hygroscopic stickiness and superior skin moisturisation at equivalent concentrations. These sensory advantages, combined with the availability of bio-based grades produced by fermentation of corn glucose, make PDO the preferred PG replacement for natural-positioned and clean-label formulations.

HydraBase PDO provides equivalent functionality to Propylene Glycol as a humectant, active solubiliser and skin feel modifier, with three commercial advantages over PG: (1) Natural-origin credentials when sourced from corn fermentation — COSMOS-eligible, clean-label compatible, PG-free positioning; (2) Superior skin feel — lighter, drier, less tacky than PG at equivalent loading, enabling higher humectant concentrations without consumer perception of stickiness; (3) Improved skin moisturisation — clinical studies comparing equivalent concentrations of PDO and PG in identical formulations consistently show higher skin hydration scores for PDO. These three advantages explain the progressive displacement of PG by PDO in premium natural and clean-label personal care.

Mechanism — 1,3-Diol vs. 1,2-Diol Structural Difference

The positional isomery between PDO (1,3-propanediol) and PG (1,2-propanediol) produces subtle but commercially meaningful differences. In 1,2-propanediol (PG), the two —OH groups are on adjacent carbons, enabling intramolecular hydrogen bonding between them — this partially quenches the individual —OH groups' capacity to interact with external water and skin surfaces. In 1,3-propanediol (PDO), the —OH groups are separated by one additional carbon, preventing the intramolecular hydrogen bond — both —OH groups remain fully available for intermolecular interaction with water and skin. This structural difference is reflected in PDO's slightly higher individual —OH group water interaction efficiency and the correspondingly improved skin moisturisation versus PG at equivalent concentration. The reduced stickiness of PDO is attributed to different geometry of surface adsorption onto skin keratin: the 1,3-diol configuration produces a less viscous surface film than the 1,2-diol configuration at equivalent mass loading.

Product Overview

AquaFilm K30 is Polyvinylpyrrolidone K30 (PVP K30) — a nonionic, water-soluble homopolymer of N-vinylpyrrolidone with molecular weight approximately 40,000–58,000 g/mol and K-value 29–32. PVP was the first synthetic polymer applied as a hair fixative (1950s), replacing shellac as the reference film former. The K-value system is a relative viscosity measure: K30 designates the medium molecular weight grade with ideal solution viscosity for spray and pump applications at 3–6% concentration in water or water-alcohol vehicles.

AquaFilm K30 forms stiff, transparent, highly glossy films on hair fibres as the aqueous carrier evaporates: the PVP chains deposit on the hair surface and form a continuous polymer coating that physically locks hair in the styled position while providing optical enhancement (increased light reflection = increased perceived shine and health). The film characteristics at low humidity are crisp and stiff; at high humidity, PVP's hygroscopicity causes film absorption of atmospheric moisture, producing softer films — a characteristic that can be mitigated by blending with less hygroscopic polymers or by using K90 (higher hold, lower humidity sensitivity per unit polymer).

Mechanism — Polymer Chain Film Formation and Styling Hold

The hair-styling film formation of PVP occurs through a concentration-driven phase transition as the solvent evaporates after product application. In solution, PVP K30 chains are independently solvated and mobile; as water (and/or ethanol) evaporates, the polymer concentration increases, chains become entangled, and eventually the polymer undergoes a glass transition — vitrifying from a mobile viscous polymer into a glassy rigid film. The lactam rings of the PVP monomer units pack into a dense, amorphous glass with a glass transition temperature well above ambient temperature, producing the characteristic crispness and stiffness of PVP films at low humidity. Styling hold is generated by the mechanical coupling of hair fibres through the glassy polymer film deposited at fibre contact points: the fibre-to-fibre adhesion resists the mechanical forces of hair movement. Hold strength scales with PVP molecular weight — higher MW = more inter-chain entanglement = stiffer, more tenacious film = greater styling hold.

Product Overview

AquaFilm K90 is Polyvinylpyrrolidone K90 (PVP K90) — the high molecular weight grade of PVP with molecular weight approximately 900,000–1,500,000 g/mol and K-value 85–95. The molecular weight is approximately 20–30× higher than K30, producing fundamentally different film-forming properties: significantly greater inter-chain entanglement, higher film modulus, stronger hair fibre adhesion and greater resistance to humidity-induced film softening. These properties translate directly to stronger styling hold — at equivalent polymer mass, K90 delivers approximately 2–3× the hold force of K30.

The primary formulation constraint of K90 is solution viscosity: at K30-equivalent concentrations (3–6%), K90 produces extremely viscous, gel-like solutions incompatible with spray applications. K90 is therefore used at lower concentrations (1–3%) where solution viscosity remains processable, or it is blended with K30 to achieve intermediate MW profiles with controlled viscosity. In gel formulations where high viscosity is desired (hair gel, wet-look gel, pomade), K90 alone at 1–3% provides both the film-forming active and significant contribution to gel body and structure.

Mechanism — High MW Chain Entanglement and Superior Film Mechanics

The superior hold performance of K90 versus K30 is directly attributable to the dramatically higher degree of polymer chain entanglement in K90 films. In polymer physics, film mechanical properties (modulus, tensile strength, toughness) scale strongly with molecular weight above the critical entanglement molecular weight — and PVP K90 (MW ~1 million) is well above this threshold while K30 (MW ~50,000) may be near or below it. The K90 glassy film formed at hair fibre contact points has a higher glass modulus (stiffer), higher tensile strength (resists deformation before breaking) and greater fracture toughness (resists crack propagation) than equivalent K30 films. The higher humidity resistance of K90 also arises from chain entanglement: in the high-MW network, water molecules absorbed at high humidity must disrupt more inter-chain entanglements to plasticise the film, requiring higher water activity (higher relative humidity) to produce the same degree of film softening compared to K30.
